# Life Insurance Knowledge Base

This knowledge base provides general educational information about life
insurance. It is not legal, financial, tax, medical, or policy-specific advice.
Exact coverage, eligibility, premiums, exclusions, and claim outcomes depend on
the policy contract and the insurer.

## Term Life Insurance

Term life insurance provides coverage for a set period, such as 10, 20, or 30
years. If the insured person dies during the active term and the claim meets the
policy terms, the insurer pays a death benefit to the named beneficiaries. Term
life is often used for temporary needs such as income replacement, mortgage
protection, or support for dependents.

## Whole Life Insurance

Whole life insurance is a permanent life insurance policy designed to last for
the insured person's lifetime if required premiums are paid. It usually includes
a guaranteed death benefit and a cash value component that can grow over time.
Whole life premiums are often higher than term life premiums for the same death
benefit amount.

## Universal Life Insurance

Universal life insurance is a permanent policy with flexible premiums and an
adjustable death benefit, subject to policy rules. It includes a cash value
component that may earn interest. Policy performance can depend on premium
payments, fees, interest crediting, and policy charges.

## Beneficiaries

A beneficiary is the person, trust, or organization named to receive the death
benefit. Policyholders can often name primary and contingent beneficiaries.
Keeping beneficiary information current is important after major life events
such as marriage, divorce, birth, or death.

## Premiums

Premiums are payments made to keep a policy active. Premium amounts may depend
on age, health, coverage amount, policy type, underwriting class, tobacco use,
term length, riders, and insurer pricing. Missing payments can cause a policy to
lapse unless a grace period, cash value feature, or other policy provision
applies.

## Death Benefits

The death benefit is the amount payable to beneficiaries when the insured person
dies and the claim is approved under the policy terms. The payment is commonly
made as a lump sum, though some insurers may offer other settlement options.

## Eligibility

Eligibility varies by insurer and policy. Common factors include age, residence,
health history, occupation, hobbies, tobacco use, financial justification, and
the requested coverage amount. Some policies require medical underwriting, while
others use simplified or guaranteed issue processes.

## Underwriting

Underwriting is the insurer's review process for deciding whether to offer
coverage and at what price. It may include application questions, prescription
history, medical records, lab work, a paramedical exam, motor vehicle records,
and financial information. Conditions such as diabetes, high blood pressure, or
heart disease do not always prevent someone from applying, but they may affect
pricing, coverage options, or approval.

## Claims Process

Beneficiaries usually start a claim by contacting the insurer or the agent who
serviced the policy. The insurer will provide claim forms and instructions.
After documents are submitted, the insurer reviews the policy status, cause of
death, beneficiary information, and any exclusions or contestability issues.
Approved claims are then paid according to the insurer's process.

## Common Claim Documents

Commonly requested claim documents include:

- Completed claim form
- Certified death certificate
- Policy number or policy document, if available
- Beneficiary identification
- Proof of relationship or trust documents, when applicable
- Assignment, estate, or court documents, if required
- Additional medical, accident, or investigation records in some cases

## Exclusions and Limitations

Life insurance policies can include exclusions and limitations. Examples may
include a contestability period, suicide clause, material misrepresentation,
policy lapse, excluded riders, or limits tied to accidental death benefits.
Actual exclusions depend on the policy language and applicable law.
